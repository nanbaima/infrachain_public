Energy Saving Scenarios

Scenario A.

On this winter day, both wind and solar energy are not strong; during the night, wind production can account for almost 50% of the mix because demand is low, but as consumption increases during the day, the renewable share decreases since the photovoltaic production is not enough to compensate. The share of renewables is higher during the night.

Scenario B.

It is again full winter, but in this case the weather conditions allow for a very strong wind production in the first part of the day; the share of renewables stays high at almost 70% throughout the day and starts to decrease slowly as the evening consumption peak, typical in weekdays, requires the generation of additional electricity from fossil fuels. The share of renewables is higher from the night to the afternoon.

Scenario C.

Now it is spring and wind production is lower than in the previous season. Photovoltaics are now stronger and dominate the renewable mix. The actual share of renewable energy in the total mix is low for the moment, reaching its peak at noon with more than 30%. This proportion will increase with the advance of summer and represents a situation in which the share of renewables is higher during the morning and afternoon.
